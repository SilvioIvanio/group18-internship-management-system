# Project Proposal

## 1. Context and Background

### 1.1 The Role of Work Integrated Learning at NUST

The Namibia University of Science and Technology (NUST) is widely recognised as Namibia’s premier institution for career-oriented higher education. Unlike traditional universities that focus primarily on theoretical knowledge, NUST emphasises practical, industry-relevant training that prepares students for immediate employment upon graduation. One of the most critical requirements for every undergraduate student at NUST is the successful completion of Work Integrated Learning (WIL), commonly referred to as an internship. This mandatory component ensures that students graduate not only with academic knowledge but also with genuine workplace experience.

Internships serve as a bridge between the classroom and the professional world. During an internship, students apply the theories and skills they have learned in real business environments, interact with experienced professionals, and develop essential soft skills such as communication, teamwork, problem-solving, and professional etiquette. Without a successfully completed internship, students are not permitted to graduate, regardless of their academic performance in coursework

### 1.2 The Cooperative Education Unit (CEU)

The Cooperative Education Unit (CEU) was officially launched at NUST in March 2011 with a clear and important mandate: to link NUST students with industry partners for Work Integrated Learning placements. Over the past fifteen years, the CEU has grown into a vital department within the university, serving thousands of students across all faculties.

The core functions of the CEU include:

- Signing Memoranda of Understanding (MoUs) with companies and organisations across Namibia
- Organising annual career fairs where students can meet potential employers
- Conducting work-readiness workshops to prepare students for professional environments
- Maintaining relationships with industry partners
- Advertising internship opportunities to students
- Matching students to suitable placements
- Monitoring internship progress and compliance

To date, the CEU has successfully signed over 230 MoUs with various industry partners, including major Namibian companies, government agencies, and non-profit organisations. Despite this impressive network, recent reports indicate that some faculties still face serious shortages of available placement spots relative to student demand (Namibian Sun, 2025).

### 1.3 Current Manual Process

Currently, the internship management process at NUST follows a largely manual workflow:
Step 1 - Advertising: When a company offers an internship opportunity, the CEU receives the details via email or physical letter. The CEU staff then post the advertisement on the CEU website (ceu.nust.na) and sometimes on physical notice boards around the campus.

Step 2 - Student Application: Interested students must visit the CEU website, read the advertisement, and then manually prepare and submit their applications. This typically involves writing a cover letter, preparing a CV, and gathering academic transcripts. Students send these documents via email to the CEU or physically deliver them to the CEU office.

Step 3 - Application Review: CEU staff collect all applications for each position, often using email folders or physical files. They review the applications and select candidates to forward to the company.

Step 4 - Placement Matching: Using spreadsheets and manual judgment, CEU staff match students to available internship positions. This process is time-consuming and subjective.

Step 5 - Notification: Students are notified of their placement status via phone call, email, or physical notice. Many students report delays or missed notifications.

Step 6 - Internship Execution: Once placed, students begin their internships. However, tracking progress is difficult because there is no central system. Lecturers and company mentors struggle to monitor student performance.

Step 7 - Evaluation: At the end of the internship, students submit physical logbooks and evaluation forms. These are often lost or delayed.

Step 8 - Reporting: CEU staff manually compile semester reports using spreadsheets, a process that takes weeks of administrative effort.

### 1.4 National Context: Youth Unemployment in Namibia

The challenges with internship placement at NUST must be understood within the broader national context of youth unemployment in Namibia. According to the Namibia Statistics Agency (2023), the country continues to face a high youth unemployment rate of 44.4%. Many young Namibians struggle to find employment after graduation, not because they lack academic qualifications, but because they lack practical work experience.

Internships are widely recognised as a critical solution to this problem. Students who complete quality internships are significantly more likely to secure full-time employment within six months of graduation. Employers also benefit because they can evaluate potential future employees in real work settings before making permanent hiring decisions.

The Government of Namibia has consistently supported internship programmes through national policies, including Vision 2030 and various National Development Plans (NDPs). These policy frameworks emphasise skills development, youth empowerment, and the use of technology to drive economic growth and employment.

## 2. Problem Statement and Motivation

### 2.1 Problem Statement

The current management of student internships at NUST is largely manual, paper-based, and fragmented. Students search for opportunities on the CEU website or through scattered emails, fill out paper forms or send CVs via email, and the CEU staff match them to placements using spreadsheets and manual judgment. Companies send placement requests by email. Lecturers have no easy way to monitor student progress during internships.

This traditional approach leads to several serious and interconnected problems:
Even though the CEU has signed over 230 MoUs with industry partners, recent reports confirm that some faculties still face serious shortages of placement spots (Namibian Sun, 2025). Without a completed internship, students cannot graduate. This causes academic and financial stress, delays graduation dates, and ultimately contributes to the persistently high youth unemployment rate in Namibia.

### 2.2 Motivation

We are strongly motivated to solve this problem for the following reasons:
Personal Experience: The problem affects thousands of NUST students every single year, including members of our own group. We have personally experienced the frustration of searching for internships, waiting for responses, and worrying about graduation requirements.

Measurable Impact: A digital system will save time, reduce stress, and create a fair and transparent process for everyone involved. Students will find placements faster. CEU staff will spend less time on paperwork. Lecturers will have better visibility. Companies will find qualified students more easily.

Learning and Skills Development: Building this system will give our team valuable, hands-on experience with modern software development, project management using Scrum, version control with Git, and stakeholder engagement. These are exactly the skills that employers in Namibia and globally are looking for.

Alignment with National Goals: The project supports NUST’s strategic goal of innovation and technology use in daily university operations. It also supports Namibia’s Vision 2030 and National Development Plans, which emphasise digital transformation, youth empowerment, and skills development.

Long-Term Potential: The system can be expanded later with advanced features such as automatic matching algorithms, integration with company HR systems, and detailed analytics on graduate employability. Our work can serve as a foundation for future improvements.

Real Difference: We believe this project will make a real and lasting difference in the lives of NUST students, staff, industry partners, and the wider Namibian community. That is a powerful motivation.

## 3. Objectives and Scope

### 3.1 General Objective

To design, develop, and implement a user-friendly, secure, and reliable Digital Internship Management System (DIMS) that streamlines the entire Work Integrated Learning (WIL) internship process for NUST students, the Cooperative Education Unit (CEU), lecturers, academic supervisors, and industry partners.

## 4. Main Stakeholders

The success of the Digital Internship Management System depends on the active engagement and satisfaction of the following key stakeholders:

### 4.1 NUST Students (Primary Users)

Role in the System: Students will register, create profiles, search for internship opportunities, submit applications, upload required documents (CV, transcripts), track their application status, submit digital logbooks during internships, and view evaluation feedback.

Benefits: Faster placement, reduced stress, transparent process, easy access to opportunities, digital record of internship experience.

Engagement Strategy: User stories derived from student interviews; prototype testing with student volunteers.

### 4.2 Cooperative Education Unit (CEU) Staff

Role in the System: CEU staff will post internship opportunities, manage incoming applications, approve or reject applications, match students to placements, assign supervisors, monitor placement progress, and generate reports.

Benefits: Much less paperwork, real-time data on all placements, automated notifications, easy report generation, better service to students and companies.

Engagement Strategy: Regular Sprint Reviews with CEU representative; backlog prioritisation input.

### 4.3 NUST Lecturers / Academic Supervisors

Role in the System: Lecturers will view students assigned to them, approve faculty-related placements, monitor student progress during internships, review digital logbook entries, provide feedback, and submit mid-term and final evaluation reports.

Benefits: Easy access to student reports and progress, no physical logbooks to collect and store, automated reminders for evaluations.

Engagement Strategy: Requirements gathering interviews; dashboard design feedback.

### 4.5 NUST Management and Faculties

Role in the System: Faculty deans and university management will use summary reports to understand internship placement rates by faculty, identify gaps, and make policy decisions to improve industry partnerships.

Benefits: Better data on graduate employability, evidence-based decision making.
Engagement Strategy: Reporting requirements gathering; final demonstration.
